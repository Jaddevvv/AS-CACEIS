The Ideal Data Ecosystem for Human Capital Valuation
at CACEIS
Part 1 - Designing from a blank sheet: data sources, architecture, analytics, and governance
for a finance-grade human capital valuation system.
The question CACEIS now faces is not whether human capital should be valued, but how — with
what data, what models, and what level of auditability. Between ISO 30414:2025 and the ESRS S1
standard under the CSRD, the regulatory bar has risen to match the rigor of financial reporting.
Asset servicing is a business whose physical balance sheet is small but whose delivered value rests
almost entirely on the expertise, judgment, and continuity of its people.
In this first part, we describe the data ecosystem we would design from scratch to measure, monitor,
and steer that value. Part 2 will then confront this ideal with the data CACEIS has actually provided.
1. Conceptual Framework for Human Capital Valuation
Traditional accounting treats salaries, training, and benefits as expenses that depress operating
income.
We propose the opposite: treat the workforce as a productive asset whose value can be estimated,
tracked over time, and tied to the creation of economic profit. Three complementary layers make
this shift operational.
●
●
●
The first layer is productive value: what each full-time equivalent adds to revenue once
external costs are removed. Human Capital Value Added (HCVA) sits squarely here and gives
a CFO a defensible per-head productivity benchmark.
The second layer is economic-profit value: how talent moves Economic Value Added (EVA)
after the cost of capital. Recent studies referenced in our background research show that HR
drivers, engagement and training investment in particular, explain more than half the
variance in EVA for service firms, with beta coefficients of 0.42 for engagement and 0.36 for
training.
The third layer is strategic-alpha value: the degree to which CACEIS' workforce lets it
outperform peers and absorb market shocks. This is the logic JP Morgan Asset Management
has productized through its Human Capital Factor: companies with stable, well-trained teams
generate measurable alpha versus standard benchmarks.
On top of these three layers we recommend a balance-sheet view using the Lev-Schwartz model,
refined with the Flamholtz probabilities of promotion and attrition.
The Human Asset Value it produces (the discounted present value of expected future earnings across
the workforce) is imperfect, as it rewards tenure more than contribution. But it gives leadership a
single figure to track, and, crucially, it makes M&A talent due diligence (the kind BNP Paribas publicly
applied to the AXA IM acquisition in 2025) a quantitative exercise rather than a narrative one.

2. Ideal Data Sources
2.1 Internal data
The backbone of the system is the HRIS record: demographics, contract type, FTE count by country
and business line, compensation, start and end dates. This is non-negotiable and must be clean
enough on day one to meet ISO 30414:2025 composition requirements. Around it, we would deploy
eight complementary internal streams.
Performance and objectives data from the OKR or performance management system connects
individual contribution to business outcomes.
●
●
Recruitment funnel data from the ATS: time-to-fill, source quality, offer acceptance rate,
90-day retention (lets us measure the cost and effectiveness of talent acquisition, not just its
activity).
Learning and development data captured in the LMS, ideally tagged against a formal skills
taxonomy, reveals where capability is being built and where it is decaying. Internal mobility
data (promotions, lateral moves, rotations) surfaces the organization's talent liquidity: a
leading indicator of both engagement and succession depth.
Engagement and sentiment data is where we would push hardest beyond the annual survey.
●
Quarterly pulse surveys combined with event-triggered micro-surveys after key milestones
(onboarding, project closure, manager change) give a signal that moves fast enough to act
on.
●
●
●
Collaboration metadata from Microsoft 365 and Teams, feeding an Organizational Network
Analysis layer, maps who collaborates with whom, how densely, and where the informal
brokers sit, without ever reading message content.
Absence and aggregated wellbeing data warn us of burnout before it shows up as
resignation.
Finally, operational performance data: error rates, SLA compliance, ticket resolution times,
assets serviced per FTE: closes the loop between human capital investment and the
client-facing quality on which CACEIS is ultimately judged.

2.2 External data
No internal dataset can tell us whether a 12% voluntary turnover rate is good or bad for a custodian
operating across France and Luxembourg. External benchmarks anchor every metric we produce. We
recommend four families of external data.
●
●
●
●
Compensation benchmarks from Mercer, Willis Towers Watson, and the McLagan survey
(financial-services specialist) tell us whether pay is competitive by job family and seniority.
Labor market data from LinkedIn Talent Insights, Glassdoor, Lightcast, and Revelio Labs show
us how quickly competitors are hiring, in which skills, and where salary pressure is building.
A unified skills taxonomy, ideally ESCO mapped to SFIA for technology roles and to the
internal CACEIS job catalog, makes skills inventories comparable across countries and against
the market.
And peer disclosures from the 2025 CSRD filings of BNP Paribas, Société Générale, State
Street, BNY , Northern Trust, and Crédit Agricole S.A. itself give us a structured peer
benchmark on every ESRS S1 indicator.
2.3 Complementary data beyond the current scope
Four further sources, less obvious but high-leverage, would round out the ecosystem.
●
●
●
●
Client-to-employee linkage data. Matching client retention and client satisfaction to the
stability of the teams that serve them is, in our view, the single most powerful analytical
move available to an asset servicer.
○
It makes the business case for human capital investment concrete: if a ten-point drop
in turnover in fund accounting correlates with a three-point rise in client retention,
the training budget writes itself.
AI-collaboration telemetry. Microsoft's 2025 annual report introduced the "human-agent
ratio" (the share of employee time augmented by AI copilots) as a core productivity
disclosure.
○
We would track Copilot adoption, prompt volume, and automation rate per job
family to measure how quickly CACEIS teams are moving up the value chain.
Knowledge capital signals. Contributions to internal knowledge bases, documented
procedures, published methodologies, and cross-training sessions all represent intellectual
capital that outlives individual employees.
○
A simple contribution index per team is a leading indicator of resilience to
departures.
Fourth, alumni data. Former employees are a predictive dataset in their own right: where
they went, how long they stayed, what their next employer paid them.
○
It reveals both the real market value of CACEIS-trained talent and the competitive
pressure building in specific functions.

3. Collection Architecture, Tools & Governance
We recommend a hub-and-spoke architecture with a dedicated Human Capital Data Lakehouse at
the center.
The HRIS (Workday or SAP SuccessFactors) and the ATS are the authoritative systems of record. They
feed the lakehouse through change-data-capture pipelines so that every update (a promotion, a
resignation, a new hire) propagates in near real time rather than in monthly batches.
The LMS, performance tools, collaboration platforms (Microsoft 365, Teams, Viva Insights),
engagement tools (Glint, Peakon, or Culture Amp), and the talent-intelligence layer (Eightfold or
Gloat) each stream into the same lakehouse through documented connectors. External data

(compensation surveys, peer filings, labor market feeds) arrives via scheduled API pulls or vendor
uploads into clearly marked external zones.
On top of the lakehouse sits a transformation layer (dbt or equivalent) that produces the canonical
tables:
●
One row per employee-month, one row per job-family-month, one row per
business-unit-month.
●
●
A semantic layer (Cube or the dbt Semantic Layer) enforces a single definition of every
metric.
"Turnover" will mean the same thing whether it surfaces in the CFO's EVA deck or in
the CHRO's board pack (ending the most common failure mode of human capital reporting
programs).
Dashboards are served through Power BI for operational users and Tableau or ThoughtSpot
for analytical power users.
Governance runs through a Human Capital Data Steering Committee chaired jointly by the CHRO and
the CFO, with permanent membership from the CDO, the DPO, the Head of Internal Audit, and the
business-unit heads.
Every critical attribute (salary, performance rating, engagement score, termination reason) has a
named business owner and a technical steward, following the BCBS 239 principles already familiar to
CACEIS' risk function.
A central data dictionary, published in Collibra or Atlan, is the single reference for definitions, lineage,
and quality rules.
4. KPIs & Analytical Capabilities
We propose a three-tier KPI framework. Each tier serves a different audience and is built from the
tier below, so that the regulatory disclosures, the financial metrics, and the predictive analytics all
rest on the same governed data.
●
●
●
Tier 1 covers regulatory disclosures: the ISO 30414:2025 requirements and ESRS S1
mandatory data points.
Tier 2 covers financial valuation, translating human capital into the CFO's language through
HCROI, HCVA, HAV, and the isolated workforce contribution to EVA.
○
A dedicated line "workforce contribution to economic profit" should appear in
internal P&L reviews alongside traditional margin analysis.
Tier 3 is where real alpha is generated: predictive attrition scores by team and role, skills-gap
indices against a two-year strategic roadmap, ONA collaboration-health indicators, an
innovation index built from cross-functional connectivity, and a CACEIS-specific Human
Capital Factor score calibrated on peer benchmarks.

Three analytical capabilities emerge from this ecosystem that CACEIS cannot produce today.
●
●
●
The first is predictive attrition at team level, with lead times of three to six months (long
enough to intervene).
The second is a client-retention-to-employee-retention correlation per servicing line, turning
the argument for human capital investment into a quantified business case rather than an HR
claim.
The third is a CACEIS HCF score comparable across business units and against peers, which
can feed both internal capital allocation and external investor communication.
5. Continuous Data Collection Strategy
Annual surveys are a diagnostic from the past. The ecosystem we describe is built to run on a
continuous rhythm with three clocks in parallel.
●
The real-time clock covers collaboration metadata, operational performance, and lifecycle
events: a hire, a departure, a promotion, a role change.
○
These flow through event streams and update the lakehouse within minutes.

CACEIS Hackathon — Deliverable 1
●
●
The weekly-to-monthly clock covers pulse surveys, engagement micro-surveys, dashboard
KPI refreshes, and attrition-model rescoring. It gives HR business partners and managers a
cadence short enough to act on.
The annual clock retains the deep diagnostic instruments: full engagement survey,
360-degree reviews, strategic workforce planning, salary review (that require reflection
rather than reaction).
What makes the system genuinely continuous is not the speed of any one feed, but the consistency
of the semantic layer across all of them: the same employee ID, the same skill taxonomy, the same
definition of turnover, whether the data arrives every five seconds or once a year.
Each metric also carries a timestamp and a version tag, so longitudinal analysis remains possible even
when methodologies evolve.
Automated data-quality checks run at ingestion: completeness, referential integrity, anomaly
detection and route exceptions to the relevant steward. This is how a data ecosystem built for
real-time analytics also becomes audit-ready for CSRD limited and reasonable assurance.
6. Technological Infrastructure
The infrastructure rests on five layers, each built on the assumption that the system will grow and be
audited.
●
●
●
●
The ingestion layer uses change-data-capture pipelines from transactional systems (Workday,
SuccessFactors, ATS), event streaming for collaboration and operational data, and scheduled
pulls for external feeds.
○
Where possible, we standardize on managed connectors (Fivetran or Airbyte) to
keep the pipeline estate small.
The storage and transformation layer is a lakehouse on Snowflake, Databricks, or Microsoft
Fabric. Given the prevalence of the Microsoft 365 stack in CACEIS' collaboration environment
(and the ONA logic that depends on it)
○
Fabric has a natural integration advantage. Transformations and tests run in dbt, with
a medallion architecture (bronze, silver, gold) that makes lineage and data quality
inspectable at every step.
The intelligence layer is an MLOps platform (Databricks ML, Azure ML, or SageMaker) hosting
the attrition-risk model, the skills-matching model, the client-retention-linkage model, and
the sentiment-analysis pipelines.
○
Every production model has a model card, a drift monitor, and a scheduled retraining
cycle: requirements that are no longer optional under the EU AI Act.
The semantic and governance layer combines a data catalog (Collibra or Atlan), a shared
metric store (Cube or dbt Semantic Layer), and an access-control matrix wired through the
corporate identity provider with role-based and attribute-based rules.

●
○

7. GDPR Compliance & Ethical Governance
A system that aggregates collaboration metadata, sentiment scores, and predictive attrition models
is, under European law, one of the most sensitive data estates a bank can operate. We treat
compliance and ethics as design constraints, not as a final review stage.
The legal basis for processing is the execution of the employment contract together with CACEIS'
legitimate interest in workforce management.
●
●
We do not rely on employee consent, which is rarely valid in an employer-employee
relationship under the EDPB's guidance.
Every processing activity is recorded in the Article 30 register and supported by a Data
Protection Impact Assessment where the risk profile requires one, which, for ONA and
predictive attrition, it always does.
Privacy-by-design is the default at every analytical layer.
●
ONA and sentiment analytics apply a minimum group size of five, so that no individual score
is ever visible.
●
●
Direct identifiers are tokenized in analytical zones, and re-identification requires a second,
logged access through a break-glass procedure.
Sensitive categories of data (health, union membership, biometric information) are either
excluded or processed only by named teams under a separate DPIA.
The EU AI Act classifies People Analytics systems used in recruitment, performance evaluation, or
promotion decisions as high-risk.
●
●
Every model that informs such a decision carries a model card documenting its training data,
input features, measured bias, and known limitations; a human-in-the-loop for every
individual decision; and an annual algorithmic audit.
We recommend an Ethics Committee: the DPO, a CHRO representative, a business ethics
officer, and an independent external member, that reviews every high-risk model before
deployment and annually thereafter.
Employee transparency is not optional. CACEIS employees should be able to see, in plain language,
which data is collected about them, which models use it, and how to contest an automated decision.

Closing note
The ecosystem described in this document is ambitious but internally consistent: every data source
has a business purpose, every metric feeds a decision, every decision is traceable to a governed
definition, and every algorithm is auditable.
It is designed to survive both a CSRD auditor and an EU AI Act inspection and, more importantly, to
give CACEIS' leadership a live reading of the asset on which its entire franchise rests.
Part 2 of this deliverable will hold this ideal against the data CACEIS has actually provided and
propose a pragmatic path from today's reality toward this target.
